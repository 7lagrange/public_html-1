Research

We are interested in developing and applying computational methods to study human genetics and diseases. Our work is at the interface between biology, statistics, and computer science. Here are four specific areas: Genome sequencing and assembly ; Genetic mapping of disease genes ; Autoimmunity and MHC ; Pharmacogenomics

Genome sequencing and assembly

Genome sequencing and assembly is at the upstream of studies in genetics and systems biology. The rapid progress in high-throughput sequencing technologies fostered many novel computation problems. We are developing methods to infer genetic variants, formulating new applications of de novo genome assembly, and making tools that facilitate designs of sequencing studies.

Genetic mapping of disease genes

Genetic mapping is a powerful approach to uncover genetic variants that cause disease or increase the risk of diseases. Classic genetic mapping methods, including family-based linkage for Mendelian diseases and population-based association for complex diseases, are based on genetic markers (which are observed in the experiments) that are correlated with the causal variants (which are not observed). Additionally, these two methods are best powered to find genetic variants from two extremes: very rare but high penetrant variants for Mendelian disease, and common but low penetrant variants for complex diseases. Now using genome-wide sequencing, we can directly observe potential causal variants in the studied individuals. We can also access variants from the previously overlooked uncommon-frequency / moderate-penetrant spectrum. We are interested to solve novel statistical problems for mapping disease genes in this context. We are developing tools for both monogenetic and polygenetic diseases that integrate statistical evidences with functional information, such as conservation, expression and methylation.

There are evidences that complex diseases are associated with many common variants with very small effects, which cannot be addressed by conventional genetic mapping approaches without astronomical number of subjects. Yet finding these variants is still valuable for advancing our understandings of biological mechanism of the diseases. We are interested in developing statistical models to aggregate small effects of individual variants through pathways and networks.

Autoimmunity and MHC

The immune response is one of the most fascinating processes in human body. The major histocompatibility complex (MHC) region is the key player in the immune system, and has been implicated in all major autoimmune diseases and many other diseases. But it is extremely polymorphic in human population and highly complex, mostly due to its crucial function of presenting pathological antigens. The high polymorphic rate and complex structure makes it one the most difficult regions to analyze from sequencing data. We are developing methods to fully describe this genomic region based on sequencing data, and apply them to better understand the genetics of autoimmune and related diseases.

Pharmacogenomics

In collaborating with the International Serious Adverse Events Consortium (iSAEC), we are conducting genome-wide association and sequencing studies of various idiosyncratic adverse drug reactions (ADR). The ultimate goal is to find major genetic variants that can effectively predict the risk or the lack of risk for developing ADRs, and therefore lead to personalized treatment. We are currently pursuing studies in serious skin rush (Stevens-Johnson Syndrome) and hypersensitivity, drug-induced liver injury (DILI), and elongate QT interval or Torsades de pointes.
